Spatial Humanities – Community Mapping with OpenStreetMap

This assignment introduced me to community mapping tools such as MapSwipe and OpenStreetMap. Both of these tools rely on users to generate content in order to improve global spatial data. This is particularly useful in areas where official mapping is limited.

By using these platforms, I learned how digital volunteers can improve accessibility and support research. This section outlines the process I undertook, the impact of my contributions, and what I learned about how these tools may shape future work.

My Process

MapSwipe

The first step was using MapSwipe, a mobile app designed to support humanitarian mapping. After downloading the app, I completed the tutorial and spent around 30 minutes identifying buildings from satellite imagery.

The interface was simple. I could swipe through tiles and tap areas where buildings were visible. At first, the task felt repetitive, but I began to understand the importance of accuracy. One challenge was distinguishing between natural and human-made structures in shaded areas. I improved by slowing down and paying closer attention to shapes and patterns.

OpenStreetMap

Next, I moved to OpenStreetMap. I created an account and explored the web interface. I chose to map my farm at home, as I am very familiar with it and could verify the accuracy of the data.

Using the online editor, I added a missing shed, corrected road alignments, added gravel trails, mapped trees, and extended the driveway. One difficulty I encountered was understanding tagging conventions. I resolved this by referring to OpenStreetMap documentation and existing map features. Over time, I became more confident using the interface.

Implications of My Contribution

My contribution has real-world impact. The data I added could help emergency services navigate the area if needed. Even though my contribution was small, many small contributions combined can significantly improve mapping data and support humanitarian efforts.

OpenStreetMap provides free, editable geographic data that can be used by anyone. For example, researchers in UCC Geography could use it to study urban development. By improving the map in my area, I contributed to a resource that supports planning, navigation, and accessibility.

Crowdsourced data changes how maps are traditionally created. Historically, mapping was controlled by governments or private companies. Platforms like OpenStreetMap democratise this process, allowing anyone with internet access to contribute. This has important implications for Digital Humanities, enabling participatory research.

Limitations and Challenges

However, this approach raises questions about reliability. Some areas may receive more attention than others, and contributors have different levels of skill. Despite this, the collaborative nature of these platforms leads to continuous improvement over time.

From a societal perspective, these tools highlight the power of collective action. Individual contributions can build valuable global resources, especially in underserved areas where official data is lacking.

What I Learned

This assignment taught me much more than I expected about how mapping works. Previously, I assumed maps were created only by professionals and large organisations. Now I understand that everyday users can play a major role.

I improved my ability to read satellite imagery, which was more difficult than I expected. It takes practice to distinguish between buildings, roads, and natural features, especially in less developed areas.

I also learned how to use OpenStreetMap editing tools, giving me a basic understanding of how spatial data is created and organised. Accuracy is extremely important, as even small mistakes can affect how others use the map.

One of the most important lessons was that small contributions add up. Even a short amount of work contributes to a much larger system with real-world impact.

Applications

The skills I gained could be useful in many areas. Academically, understanding spatial data can be applied to geography and data analysis. Since maps are widely used in research, knowing how they are created helps evaluate their reliability.

Outside of academics, there are practical uses. In sport, especially cycling, mapping is very important. Tools like OpenStreetMap can be used to plan routes, analyse terrain, and find safer roads. This could improve training and performance.

There is also potential to continue contributing to OpenStreetMap. Even small updates, such as adding buildings or correcting roads, help keep maps accurate. With more experience, I would like to try advanced tools like JOSM, although it seems complex at the moment.

Future of Crowdsourced Mapping

Crowdsourced mapping is likely to become more important in the future. As technology improves, new ways of collecting and sharing spatial data will emerge, including real-time updates and integration with AI.

One key area is disaster response. If people on the ground can quickly update maps, emergency services can respond more effectively. This shows that mapping is not just about geography, but also communication and coordination.

Conclusion

This assignment showed that mapping is something anyone can contribute to, not just experts. It has real-world applications across many fields, and the skills learned are valuable both academically and practically.
